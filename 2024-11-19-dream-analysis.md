Dream
What is a dream?
A dream is ‘a series of images, thoughts, and emotions, often with a story-like quality, generated by mental activity during sleep; the state in which this occurs.’ Dreams typically occur in REM (Rapid Eye Movement) sleep and are made up of irrelevant fragments. There are three elements that make up the dream: the visual episode, the emotional episode, and the cognitive integration of the visual and emotional episode. Dreams usually last 9 to 30 minutes and may include visual imagery, taste, smell, hearing, or pain.

What is dream analysis?
Oxford Dictionary claims “Dream interpretation is a technique, originally used in psychoanalysis but now also used in some other psychotherapies, in which the content of dreams is interpreted to reveal underlying motivations or symbolic meanings and representations. “ 

History of the dream and dream interpretation
The history of dream interpretation can be dated back to 3100BC in Mesopotamia, ancient Egypt, and Greece, where dreaming was considered as a way of communicating with the divines. Dream interpretation was also recorded in documents, literary texts, and even funeral texts. 
The ‘founder of psychoanalysis,’ Sigmund Freud, started experimenting with the hypnosis method to treat patients.  Freud then favoured the ‘free association’ after he encountered problems with the hypnosis method. 
Later, Freud’s idea of the psychoanalysis method began to gain popularity in the psychology industry. In today’s psychology, the psychoanalytic method has become an indispensable concept in the psychology industry. 

The psychodynamic side of the approach – Freud’s perspective: ‘dreams as a royal road to the unconscious’
The Freud’s method of dream interpretation - free association
Freud ‘s method of dream interpretation has been the ruling method of psychoanalytic therapy in modern psychology. He first discovered the hypnosis method by encountering the cathartic procedure of Joseph Breuer, a physician. Freud believed repressed emotions cannot be relieved under hypnosis as patients are not brought into deep sleep. 
Freud developed another idea called ‘free association’ and abandoned the idea of hypnosis. Free association is the concept of a psychoanalytical practice where patients are asked to describe what has come to their minds during the therapy session, hoping the fragments of repressed emotions will combine during the process. Freud sees the method of free association as a way of orbiting the defence mechanism to reveal a patient’s traumatic events stored in the unconscious. This allows the patients to face their ‘emotional pain’ and lead them to their ideal selves. He also found that free association fulfills his initial aim of revealing repressed emotions via talking freely to individuals.  This type of technique only works successfully in a two-way process – not only is the patient required to reveal all their thoughts at the moment, but it is also critical for the therapist to direct and prompt the conversation during the process.  

Below is how Freud’s free association works:
	•	Patients were to sit on anything that imposed relaxation
	•	Patients were to speak freely of anything that they thought of at any time
	•	The speaking flow of the patient is not controlled, and no intervention should be made
	•	For example, a free association could look like the case below: 
I dreamt of frying pans, and they change shape constantly. I might be obsessed with the frying pans, and I want to digest them. I think there is also a need to add some salt to the frying pan. This apple looks delicious, and I have lots of thoughts about my mental well-being which does not seem to be making sense. Everybody should love chemistry and in fact, we all have a psychologist in our inner self. 
	•	Psychotherapists are then to interpret the meaning of the information given. 
         
Modern dream analysis
Freud was well known for his establishment of the dream analysis method and this method made up the fundamental concept of modern psychoanalytic therapy. His theory marks the paradigm shift of psychology in the 19th century and 20th century  One key example is the psychodynamic approach where most of its concepts are heavily drawn from Freudian theory. Freud’s free association is the forerunner of the modern transcript talking therapies. The modern psychoanalysis, initially set up in the USA between World War I and II, defines itself as a treatment that is free from constraint, which again is the crucial concept of Freudian free association. 
The modern psychoanalysis is identified as ‘an approach to the mind, personality, and psychological treatment’. One of the assumptions of modern psychoanalysis is that ‘much of the mental activity is unconscious and we have to interpret patients’ manifest behaviour to understand the unconscious.’ Different from traditional psychoanalysis, modern psychoanalysis focuses on the patient’s unique experiences rather than that of acting as an authority figure and judging the reliability of the information patients hold. Freud discovered free association, and the modern world adopted the theory just as how modern physics adopted the general law from Newton. These types of therapy are free from the length of the session – it could be short or long term. They are also for all ages, ethnicities, races, genders and sexualities. The psychoanalytic approach had achieved a truly nominal ‘free association’. 

Case study – Hilda Doolittle (H.D)
One famous case study conducted by Sigmund Freud was known as Hilda Doolittle, H.D. She was an American poet and novelist who experienced tumultuous relationships and trauma during World War I. She decided to seek help from Freud at the age of 47 and received therapy session from him.
H.D. undertook 2 periods of analyses with Freud which initiate from March to May 1933, and terminated in October and November 1934. H.D. kept a diary throughout the therapy sessions which was significant as it showed the analysand's perspective from receiving a therapy session and has become very acquainted with psychoanalysis.  
In H.D.’s letter we know that the initial session was difficult to overcome. In her letter, she claimed she was ‘so scared by Oedipus,’ she ‘cried so she could hardly utter’ and ‘she was disappointed and is disappointed in him.’ She relies mostly on him by telling Freud ‘But do you not realise you are everything, you are priest, you are magician’. In the first session she had cried over half an hour, and she suggested ‘it was terrible’. Freud was simply a ‘ghost’ who is ‘not there at all’. 
After nine sessions, she began to trust Freud and recovered rapidly. She calls Freud ‘papa’, showing their deep connection in between. She claimed her ‘transference has taken place’ and that Freud was very sweet and told her ‘she was very clever’. 
We however are unable to find other later sources of her perspective in therapeutic sessions. According to the available sources she has recovered in a very short time but relapsed again after the two therapeutic sessions she attended. Nevertheless, it still is valid evidence of Freud using the free association method to cure patients for the short term, as it provided a systematic way of exploring the unconscious mind through dreams. 
One of the critiques regarding the research is that it is a case study. H.D.’s study is unable to provide a representative image of how dream analysis cures patients across the world, in fact not even across an area of a country as it is ideographic. There are also no similar studies to H.D.. Perhaps Freud’s dream therapy is effective in providing insights into H.D. ’s mental thought process as a patient, it failed to give a nuanced explanation of how dream analysis aid people generally.  
		
Credibility of Freud’s dream analysis and theory
Freud insisted that “Psychoanalysis is part of the mental science of the psychology...Psychology too, is a natural science” throughout his career. It is interesting that Freud insisted psychoanalysis is a science but failed in proving his dream interpretation as scientific.
If this is the case, why does Freud’s dream interpretation matter? If it is considered as a pseudoscience by numerous psychologists; and if it is a theory that no empirical evidence, meaning no objective data can be collected? Should we reconsider the scientific credibility lies behind Freud’s great contribution to dream analysis? The answer is two-folded: on one side, the appearing of the dream interpretation had made an impact to the psychology industry. With many of its ideas influencing the treatment methods and deepened the understanding of psychology within the public, Freud has revolutionised the way people learned about mental health and the unconscious. Until today, nothing that Freud had written about psychoanalysis is obsolete; the psychoanalysis has grown and is still growing continuously based on the rudimentary notion of his theory. Lots of famous dream theories such as Jung’s dream theory of ’dreams reveal more than they conceal‘was alternated from Freud’s dream theory. Several others developed new theories in opposition to Freud’s ideas. Freud’s unique way of investigating dreams into unconscious has furnished countless psychologist to follow this path and discover the connection in between.
Despite all the liberal formations Freud had inspired psychologists with, his theory has encountered several issues that even modern psychology cannot resolve. The first and most significant one could be considered as dream interpretation as a pseudoscience. To meet the scientific criteria a theory must fulfil the requirement of falsification; and to meet the criteria of falsification a theory must then also fulfil the requirement of being able to collect empirical evidence.  
In this situation Freud’s theory cannot prove it is scientific as Freud cannot manipulate one of the variables; the unconscious mind is not measurable; it is not even clear if the unconscious exists. The unconscious only exists because we created a word for information we cannot access, not because it is a ’thing’ that was proven to exist. If one of the variables cannot prove its existence, no information can be gathered. Freud assumed ’dreams are the royal roads to the unconscious’ without properly establishing evidence of existence of unconscious mind. Furthermore, in Freud’s free association method, a person’s dream is reported to the therapist in a self-report way, which hugely increased the inaccuracy and bias of the whole section. Likewise, different therapist interprets the same dream in diverse ways, for example different therapist might interpret the information of ‘falling off a building’ differently. With these figures in mind, it is possible that Freud’s idea was entirely wrong from the beginning and that dreams cannot provide a valid understanding of our ’unconscious’ mind.

Freud’s dream theory has always been criticised for alpha bias. According to Freud, the disorder hysteria, which is closely related to dreams only attributed to women. His views maximised the difference between men and women, without acknowledging the fact that there are some similarities between women and men. This is a problem as it assumed women and men experience different psychological developments when growing up and therefore will lead to stereotypes about assumption of men and women. These views are extremely out-of-date and is still an issue today and hence has low temporal validity.

We cannot tell if people in individualist countries act the same way as people in collectivist countries as Freud did not conduct a study on people in collectivist countries.

The psychodynamic side of the approach –the Jungian perspective – collective unconscious in dreams

The Jungian’s method of dream interpretation
Jung’s dream theory was influenced by Freud’s dream interpretation method. While Freud has a more quasi conception of the unconscious, Jung came up with an idea of a form of idealism with his ‘hermeneutic circle’ of analytical psychology. Jung is one of the founders of modern psychotherapy and his creative therapeutic techniques made him the founder of art therapy. According to Jung, dreams should be the way to lead an individual to the ‘dialogue between ego and the self ‘.  Jung objected Freud ‘s idea of ‘dreams are meant to be secretive'.  Jung did not believe dreams are needed to be interpreted to act out their function, he instead believed dreams integrate our conscious and unconscious lives - the process of individualisation. Jung has provided the most comprehensive view of dynamics of psyche yet available. 

The goal of Jung’s dream analysis is referred as individualisation, which is another word for self-actualisation. This process involves moving forward the manifestation of all the elements of personality. Jung conveys that dreams take us to the world we do not pay attention to, and that is the key aspect of achieving individualisation. 
The analytical method in Jung’s theory works the same as Freud’s dream interpretation; the patients lie in a comfortable position and start talking about their dreams, whereas the therapist takes notes of their dreams and annotates them. In analytical psychology, archetypes, which its meaning will be explained later, play a significant role in assisting patients to understand the several aspects of personality. 

Exploring the collective unconscious
The collective unconscious is made up of mostly the archetypes. Archetypes are ’any set of symbols representing aspects of the psyche that derive from the human experience.’  Jung believed there is no limit to the number of archetypes we own, yet there are four major archetypes that are universal for all humans. If the collective unconscious does not owe the personal experience, I therefore produced the first assumption: that the collective unconscious exists in another psyche system which is universal for everyone and that the ’another psyche system’ has not yet been discovered by humans.

Issues faced in Jungian therapy
One of the biggest critiques is patient bias. Patient subjectivity is especially the case in Jung’s analytical psychology. Since it follows the principle of ‘patients speak whatever they came into mind and therapist annotates’, it is affected by the mood, feelings, and ideas of the patients. For example, if the patient is not in a good mood when attending the therapy, the analyst is unlikely to not be influenced by their mood and stay professional. 
Another critique is the report of feelings and information utilises the method of self-report. The self-report method is highly based on the information given by the patients. It is extremely subjective and the risk of inaccuracy due to memory decay is high likewise, especially when dreams are not a piece of easy information to remember. Dreams are fragments that doesn’t make sense and becomes vague when we wake up. Furthermore, there is no prove whether the patient’s information is accurate, as it is impossible to assess the truth ‘level’ of their speech. At this point it is really based on the skilfulness of the analyst to interpret whether their speech is truth or not.
In analytical therapy, the analysts often make use of questionnaires to interpret patients’ dreams, this is another form of self-report. Another downside of self-report is that it depends on how the individual interpret the information. For example, if a Likert scale is used in a questionnaire asking patients to rate the accuracy of reflection of unconscious in dreams and a patient rated 9, it might be interpreted differently amongst the analyst and the patient. Even the most skilful analyst interprets the information differently to the other analysts, and this is inevitable because everyone thinks in a distinct way and is influenced by different environments. 

Cultural bias in Jungian theory
Jung's theory is highly gender and culturally biased. He has made generalisations about the psychology of Africans and women without conducting studies on them. In Jung’s Psychological types he suggests women are naturally inclined towards feeling and relational thinking, while men were predisposed to rationality.  Whilst Jung’s great contribution is seen in his various case studies, his generalisations have challenged his scientific credibility of the theory. 
Jung could also be identified as ‘racist’. His thinking became racist when he equates the states of mind with those ‘primitive’ people, i.e. white Americans. He sees the psyches of the Africans as less developed and inferior to those of white people. This impacts the validity of the theory as in this case, the theory might have been established out of the norms of white people. In this situation Jung might have interpret the psyche of the Africans wrongly, thinking they are ‘less developed’ as the Americans. But in reality, it could be because the psyche is developed differently in Africa and America, just as how the psyche is also developed differently in China and America because they are collectivist and individualist countries respectively. This affects how the dream approach is interpreted by Jung’s followers across the world and thus has low internal validity.

The scientific aspect of the dream interpretation – Cognitive Behavioural Dream Analysis (CBDA)

Intertwining with CBT (Cognitive Behavioural Therapy)

The Cognitive Behavioural Dream Analysis (CBDA) is an approach that integrates with Cognitive Behaviourist Therapy (CBT). It is sometimes combined with CBT because their principles are so similar. The CBDA is often referred as cognitive behavioural psychotherapy. 

The CBDA is based on the ‘theoretical conception of dreaming and intertwines with the principles and methodology of the CBT.’  Because CBT and CBDA are so similar, I from now on will explain the dream aspect of CBT rather than CBDA. This is also because CBDA is so unpopular and that no sources can be found regarding its function. I will now focus on the clinical perspective of the usage of dreams in CBT in the following paragraphs. 

CBT has quite a unique progress of applying dreams to psychotherapy. It was not until the early 21st century when the CBT therapist started to consider using dreams in therapies. They abandoned Freud’s theories and approached Alder’s position who believed ‘dream was a representation of dreamer’s world and lifestyle.’ 

A reductionist approach

CBT is often being criticised as a reductionist approach as it only focuses on specific and observable components in a dream. CBT breaks down the whole dream into its constituent parts and focuses on the thinking patterns of the patient rather than the meaning of the whole dream of an individual. It overlooks what the dream has talk about human unconscious such as human psyche or unconscious motives and has oversimplified other aspects of a dream such as their meaning and feelings experienced during dreaming, yet sometimes it is the dream itself or the feelings that plays an important role in CBT dream analysis. Therefore, CBT might not be the best approach to present a valid understanding of human mind.

Rather than focusing on individual’s dreams, CBT therapist tends to gather information from numerous clients and make assumptions across all clients who has the same disorder. It also has a structured, standardised treatment which leads to a “one-size-fits-all" approach that does not take account into an individual’s dreams. CBT dream analysis is as broad as the myth ‘if you dream of you falling down the building you are stressed’. This does not help give insights into human mind. 

In CBT dream analysis, clients also become the active agent to talk about their dreams whereas CBT therapists note down and annotate the information given. This means self-report and memory accuracy plays a significant role in this situation. Yet as we mentioned earlier, self-reports are extremely unreliable as they are based on memories they hold during the time they describe their dreams; and usually by the time they report their dreams to the CBT therapist, several days or even weeks have gone, this means memory inaccuracies has increased and hence we cannot interpret whether the dreams the patients record are reliable or not. Self-report technique is also objective, clients might only report those of the information they think are ‘normal’ to report and keep the ‘abnormal’ fragments to themselves, this gives a limited positive effect to the CBT dream analysis can bring to the patient. 

Short term focus of symptom relief

Another aspect we must acknowledge before generating a conclusion of whether CBT provides a valid understanding of the human mind is that the CBT dream analysis only engages with the symptoms that are present during the therapeutic session. Rather than also focusing on the client’s past and future, CBT only cures symptoms that are seen at the moment. Of course, focusing on the present is the foremost treatment method a therapist could contribute to a patient, yet this focus might neglect the traumatic past or uncertain future, which doesn’t make CBT effective as ignoring the past and future will not give a full explanation of why the patient behave this certain way. 

How far does CBDA (CBT) provide a valid understanding of one’s human mind?

Surely CBT is reliable as a psychoanalysis treatment, but is it still as effective when it comes to CBT dream analysis?  The answer is possibly not at all. Dream analysis is only effective when the analyst is professional enough to correctly interpret the deep meaning of the client’s dream; and when the analysand is willing to give up social desirability to perceive even the ‘abnormal’ parts of their dreams to the analyst – which is not yet possible. Even the most knowledgeable analyst cannot elucidate dreams without any mistakes as dreams are interpreted differently by everyone. CBT itself has a significant position in the psychology industry, yet its dream analysis needs to improve its evidence collecting methodology to own a place in todays’ psychology. Hence it could be argued CBT dream analysis method cannot provide a valid understanding of human mind.

Is dream analysis valid and accurate? Can it be justified?
The validity of the dream analysis is the topic of debate throughout the centuries of field of psychology. Method employed by Freud, Jung and CBT rely on subjective interpretations of self-reported data. And the mental process underpinning dreams is not falsifiable. However, evidence of Freud and Jung’s methods of dream analysis has shown analysis in dreams to be useful in therapy and therefore suggests dream analysis has its place in clinical psychology, despite validity issues. In order to fully answer my question, we must go beyond the theoretical framework and consider the biological, life and societal influences. Dreams can be influenced by lots of aspects, and therefore we could say a dream is ‘mobile’ and ‘flexible’. It is mobile in a way that even if humans dream of the same thing it does not provide the same meaning. To fully understand human mind through dreams, therapists must also acknowledge dreams are mobile and interpret them flexibly. 
Dream interpretation, however, should not be considered as accurate. In fact, all types of dream interpretations should not be considered accurate since most of the theories operate the self-report technique as the main method to understand patients' dreams. Self-report technique is biased, objective and is not reliable, it is almost impossible to know if the information given is accurate or not; the only thing we could do is to assume they are the truth. This increases the risk of information being unreliable and therefore lacks accuracy.
But can dream analysis be justified? The answer is two-folded. On one hand, the usage of self-report technique makes it impossible to be justified, since the method itself cannot be modified. Yet, on the other hand, it does bring patients better personal growth and understand more about their unconscious motives. It is also a prominent method used in modern talking therapies. While dream analysis may lack accuracy and is identified as a pseudoscience, it can still be justified as a valuable tool in therapies. The only way to access dreams currently is also via self-report technique, and hence it can be justified.
Can dream analysis provide a valid understanding of the human mind?
Lots of psychological approaches have attempted to use dreams to understand the human mind. Arguably, none can be considered truly valid due to issues with the methods used to investigate dreams. Having evaluated different approaches to the usage of dream interpretation in psychoanalysis, I have come to the conclusion that dream analysis can provide a valid understanding of the human mind, but only to a certain extent.  It can certainly provide us insights into unconscious mind, such as how our traumatic experiences lead to our behaviour, or when exploring human emotions and conflicts. However, whilst dream analysis can provide a valid understanding of the human mind, it might not be able to provide a valid understanding, due to lots of challenging aspects it has been facing, First, the self-report technique – a unreliable technique is used when dream content is given to the analyst, this is also the only way possible to observe dreams. Second, the unconscious is not measurable, even with the well-developed scientific technique, the unconscious is still not observable due to the fact that it lives in the deepest of the conscious mind, and I suspect the unconscious will not be able to observe in anytime, therefore making dream interpretation extremely difficult to evaluate and becomes valid.
Though dream analysis still lacks key aspects to become a scientific approach, it can still provide a certain aspect of a valid understanding of the human mind.
