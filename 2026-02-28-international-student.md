# Not Quite Here, Not Quite There: Reflections from an International Psychology Student



‘Not quite here, and not quite there.’ This phrase resonates with a familiar experience for many international students. Although embedded within a United Kingdom academic institution, international students often feel distant, isolated, and disconnected from the everyday social environment that shapes a sense of belonging. The identity for an international psychology student often switches from a ‘Psychology student’ to an ‘International student’; international students might even categorise themselves as someone who does not fully fit either of these. This ‘in-between’ position reflects a sense of liminality – the idea of ‘betwixt and between’ (Williams, 2001), where international students navigate their identities and belonging during periods of transition.  

Liminality does not simply describe transition in international students – it captures the psychological experience of existing between two, or even multiple, established identities. For international psychology students, they are primarily psychology students engaging with research; in other contexts, they are rather defined by their cultures and nationalities. The constant shift of self can be very disorienting. Tajfel & Turner (2004) proposed the Social Identification Theory (SIT), which argues that individuals define part of their self-concept and self-esteem based on the social groups they belong to. In the case of international students, they often belong to multiple groups, for example, a group from their own culture and a group from their major, meaning their identities constantly shift through interactions and their adaptations to the groups they align with. International students are then ‘stuck in the middle’, as this multiplicity might create not only opportunity, but also tension for them. While belonging to multiple social groups broadens students’ cultural awareness, it also leads to identity ambiguity (Marginson, 2014), especially when international students feel only partially recognised and adapted to all their social groups. When this sense of belonging is fragmented and incomplete, international students might feel uncertain about where they fully fit and belong (Grimshaw & Sears, 2008). In this sense, liminality is not just about transitioning from one place to another, but more about negotiating psychologically how to feel included. 

This sense of inclusion is also not solely limited to group membership. International students can be physically present in academic institutions while feeling psychologically peripheral (Bradley, 2000) – and this feeling is often a barrier to networking, communication styles, academic performance and representation of voices from international students (Poyrazli & Grahame, 2007), which results in a sense of being ‘on the margins’ of informal influence and recognition from local students. SIT suggests belonging requires recognition from the ‘in-group,’ i.e., the group they belong to. With the partial recognition from the in-group, emotional impact extends beyond social discomfort to self-doubt, which puts international students at a higher risk of having poor mental wellbeing (Alshammari et al., 2023). Combining with the idea of liminality, belonging is then both challenging in terms of emotional and psychological experience. 

Cultural transition extends beyond social adjustment; it also shapes how psychological knowledge is interpreted by international students. When international psychology students engage with different theories, the process can be subtle both culturally and linguistically. Fundamental concepts and theories in psychology are often based on Western beliefs (Mpofu, 2002), for example, the concept of individualism in attachment theories, and perhaps even the behaviour of help-seeking in mental wellbeing might differ between cultures. Psychology does not just reveal cultural differences socially – it also exposes how theories that are claimed to be universal might not always be universal (Brock, 2016). As a result, learning psychology, especially from an international student’s perspective, can become a ‘silent comparison’ through holding one’s own cultural frameworks alongside those theories. This comparison can then be cognitively and emotionally demanding. In this way, the experience of being ‘present’ in the classroom does not always equal feeling fully represented by the knowledge.  

However, this feeling of ‘not quite here, not quite there’ might cultivate resilience. Experiences of displacement might become self-awareness and eventually a supporting source throughout their academic journey, rather than uncertainty, as time progresses. Research by McKeering et al. (2021) indicated that international students who have spent 0-2 years outside their home country had lower resilience than those who spent a longer time outside. This means adapting to a new environment is gradual and is just a matter of time – what initially feels like uncertainty may become a foundation for confidence and competence over time. Psychology helps balance those negative feelings, as concepts like identity development and belonging offer a ‘language’ to understand why these negative feelings take place. For international students, navigating unfamiliar academics fosters independence, adaptability, and the confidence to advocate for themselves within society. Learning to speak up for themselves, seek clarification, or voice alternative cultural perspectives becomes not just an academic skill, but an act of self-definition. In this way, the in-between space is not just a trigger of tension; it can also become a space of growth, perspective, and strength.  

Why do international students matter, and why does this reflection matter for the world of psychology? First, we must acknowledge that the goal of psychology is to benefit wider society and enhance human lives - psychologists also examine the relationships between brain function, behaviour, and the environment to improve our world. (American Psychological Association, 2013) International students do not just adapt to the existing psychological society, but they contribute to and reshape it through offering alternative approaches to the existing Western framework (Tan, 2025). By bringing diversity of mental health approaches and identity, those communities that remain culturally narrow expand. Perspectives and assumptions shaped previously by Western cultures are challenged by collectivist values and diverse identities. This being said, the sense of ‘not quite here, not quite there’ is, in fact, more than just transitional, but rather intellectually generative – psychology benefits when different voices that move across cultural contexts are listened to, as suggested by Harrell & Bond (2020) through their principle of community psychology, because it allows us collectively to reflect critically on what is presented as universal. In this way, embracing international voices is not simply an act of inclusion but a significant step forward in building a psychology that truly reflects the world we are in.  

The feeling of ‘not quite here, not quite there’ should not be understood as a deficit, but rather a perspective that moves forward the current position of psychology. If psychology seeks to understand behaviour under all cultural complexity, it must create opportunities for international students, where their voices should not only be included, but actively heard. This is achieved through promoting diverse backgrounds, amplifying underrepresented voices – in doing so, psychology will move closer to becoming a truly inclusive society. When international perspectives are valued as integral contributions rather than additions, psychology becomes more reflective of the global communities it is serving. Embracing these in-between voices of international students strengthens the discipline and capacity to respond effectively to an increasingly interconnected world
